An interactive closure instrument

Lehmer's Box

Two walls — a golden floor at φ and an angle lattice at the four cardinal directions — fence in everything the construction can emit. Compose seeds with the spectral operators and watch the result stay inside the box. Then throw a Salem number at it and watch it fall outside. The mathematics is computed live: roots, Mahler measures, and on-circle arguments, all from the integer polynomials themselves.

01

The instrument

The scope plots every root of the current element in the complex plane. On the unit circle sit four glowing posts — the directions 0, π/2, π, 3π/2. A root on the circle is the only kind the walls constrain: it must land on a post. The gauge below shows the Mahler measure against the floor φ. Membership in the box is floor ∧ walls.

The closure lab

Pick two elements (seeds, or things you've already emitted) and an operator. ⊕ direct sum unions the spectra; ⊗ tensor multiplies eigenvalues pairwise; (·)² squares them. Each emission is analysed live, loaded into the scope, and added to the log. The point is the running tally: compose as deeply as you like and nothing ever leaves the box.

04

The intruders

These numbers are not in the catalog and cannot be emitted. Load one and watch the scope: a Salem number's on-circle conjugates scatter to irrational angles, missing every post. The instrument reports exactly which wall fails.

The subtle case is β₄. It is a genuine Salem number with measure 1.7220838, which is above the floor φ = 1.6180340 — it clears the height wall. Yet it is still outside the box, because its on-circle pair sits at the irrational angle 0.726π, off every post. This is why the box excludes all Salem numbers, not merely the ones below φ: the lattice wall, not the floor, is what does the work.
05

Why the walls hold: the angle law

The operators move arguments by two rules only: ⊗ adds them, (·)² doubles them. The four posts (π/2)ℤ are closed under both — but not under halving. No operator halves an angle, so no operator can manufacture a new direction. Try it.

Pick an operation. The result lands on the lattice — or, for halving, between two posts at π/4, a direction no operator can reach.

A Salem number's on-circle conjugate carries exactly such an off-lattice angle (irrational, by irreducibility of its minimal polynomial). The emission walls keep every on-circle eigenvalue on a post, so a Salem conjugate can never appear — and the box omits every Salem number.

06

What this shows, and what it doesn't

◆ What the instrument proves

The seven seeds lie in the box, and the spectral operators preserve both walls, so the entire emission image is confined: every measure lands in {1} ∪ [φ, ∞).

No Salem number can be emitted: its on-circle conjugates are off-lattice, so it fails the angle wall regardless of its measure. The whole band (1, μ_S) is therefore absent from the image, and the cost floor λ·log φ > 0 holds.

◆ What it leaves untouched

It says nothing about Salem numbers as numbers. Lehmer's number still exists and may, for all anyone has proved in ninety years, sit arbitrarily close to 1. It is excluded as an emission, never as a number.

So this is a sidestep, not a resolution. Lehmer's problem stays exactly where it was; the construction simply never goes to fetch the numbers that would threaten its floor.
